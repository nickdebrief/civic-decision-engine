# CREF Framework Statement

## What CREF Is

CREF is the Civic Record Evaluation Framework.

CREF is a deterministic administrative analysis framework for evaluating the structure, continuity, traceability, and evolution of civic records. It provides a method for asking what can be concluded from visible record information without relying on undocumented assumptions, hidden context, or discretionary interpretation.

CREF is the methodology. The Civic Decision Engine is one software implementation of that methodology.

## Why CREF Exists

Civic records often exist across fragmented documents, institutional replies, timestamps, references, versions, procedural events, supporting materials, and administrative responses. These materials may be incomplete, distributed across different sources, or difficult to evaluate consistently.

CREF exists to evaluate what the record itself can show. It separates visible record structure from unsupported interpretation. Its purpose is to make record-based conclusions explicit, repeatable, and reviewable without requiring hidden institutional access or privileged data.

## What CREF Evaluates

CREF evaluates visible civic record information, including:

- record structure
- record metadata
- evidence presence
- evidence coverage
- conditions
- signals
- findings
- administrative pathways
- record evolution
- traceability
- reviewability
- readiness
- completeness
- sufficiency
- consistency
- integrity
- reliability
- certification
- accreditation
- auditability
- reproducibility
- transparency
- accountability

These evaluations are derived from the record and its visible supporting information.

## What CREF Does Not Evaluate

CREF does not determine:

- truth
- guilt
- innocence
- liability
- intent
- causation
- motive
- legality
- wrongdoing
- institutional fault
- factual correctness

CREF evaluates records, not ultimate facts. It does not decide whether an event is true, whether a party is responsible in law, or whether an institution acted properly. It evaluates what can be shown from the visible record.

## Inputs

Typical CREF inputs include:

- record reference
- version
- timestamps
- supersession relationships
- verification hashes
- generated_by markers where available
- conditions
- signals
- findings
- supporting evidence
- administrative responses
- record metadata

CREF does not require hidden institutional access, privileged data, undisclosed records, or private decision-maker context. If information is not visible in the record, CREF does not invent it.

## Outputs

Typical CREF outputs include:

- classifications
- summaries
- reviews
- coverage statements
- sufficiency findings
- traceability findings
- integrity findings
- reliability findings
- accountability visibility statements

CREF outputs are derived from record structure and declared evaluation rules. They are not expressions of opinion, prediction, preference, or institutional endorsement.

## Core Guarantees

### Determinism

The same visible inputs produce the same outputs. CREF classifications and reviews follow declared rules rather than discretionary judgment.

### Visibility

CREF uses only visible record information. It does not rely on hidden records, private institutional access, undisclosed assumptions, or inferred facts.

### Reproducibility

Another observer using the same visible information should be able to derive the same visible conclusions. CREF is intended to support independent review of the record analysis.

### Non-Mutation

CREF analysis never alters the record being evaluated. It does not create, edit, repair, delete, supersede, publish, or reclassify records. The record remains separate from the analysis.

## Evidence Evaluation

CREF treats evidence as recorded information. It evaluates whether evidence exists, whether it is covered, whether it is sufficient for review, whether it can be traced, and whether it supports the administrative analysis.

CREF does not certify evidence as true. It does not decide whether evidence proves an ultimate fact. It evaluates the visible evidence structure and the role that evidence plays within the record.

## Record Evolution

Civic records evolve over time. A record may be created, revised, superseded, exported, verified, linked to supporting evidence, or reviewed through administrative processes.

CREF evaluates record evolution through:

- summary
- continuity
- change log
- trajectory
- relationships
- traceability
- coverage
- review
- readiness
- completeness
- sufficiency
- consistency
- integrity
- reliability
- certification
- accreditation
- auditability
- reproducibility
- transparency
- accountability

Together, these concepts form the lifecycle requirements of a trustworthy civic record. They show whether the record's visible evolution can be followed, reviewed, repeated, understood, and held structurally accountable without relying on hidden context.

## Administrative Analysis

CREF produces structured administrative analysis from records. It separates:

- what is recorded
- what is missing
- what is traceable
- what is reviewable
- what is reproducible
- what is accountable

This separation is central to the framework. It prevents missing information from being concealed by summary conclusions and prevents analysis from being treated as part of the record itself.

## Framework Principles

CREF is guided by these principles:

- Records must remain separate from analysis.
- Analysis must be derived, not invented.
- Missing information must remain visible.
- Classifications must follow declared rules.
- The same inputs must produce the same outputs.
- The framework must not infer hidden facts.
- Accountability must arise from visible record structure, not accusation.

## Scope and Limitations

CREF is not a court.

CREF is not a regulator.

CREF is not an investigator.

CREF is not a truth engine.

CREF does not replace legal, institutional, or evidential decision-making. It does not decide rights, liabilities, remedies, misconduct, or factual truth. It provides a structured method for evaluating the record itself.

## Relationship to the Civic Decision Engine

The Civic Decision Engine is a software implementation of CREF.

CREF can be described, studied, critiqued, and applied independently of that software. The methodology does not depend on a particular programming language, database, user interface, deployment model, or implementation stack.

The software may render CREF outputs, but the framework is the underlying method: a deterministic way to evaluate what visible civic records can and cannot support.

## Central Question

What can be concluded from the record itself, and only from the record itself?
